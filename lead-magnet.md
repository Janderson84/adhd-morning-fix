## Trick 1: The Point Method (Stop Repeating Yourself)

**The problem:** You ask your kid to get dressed. Then you ask again. Then you
say it louder. By the fourth time, you're yelling — and you hate that you're
yelling.

**What's actually happening:** ADHD brains tune out verbal instructions. It's
not defiance. It's auditory processing. Repeated words become background noise
within seconds.

**The fix:** Walk over to the visual cue (the checklist, the chart, the clothes
laid out). Point at it. Say nothing. Wait three seconds.

This sounds ridiculous the first time you do it. But here's why it works: you've
removed the verbal processing demand. You've made it visual. You've made it *the
chart's* instruction, not *your* instruction. Your kid isn't defying you —
they're just following the next visual cue.

**Try this tomorrow:** The first time you're about to repeat yourself, stop.
Walk to the visual. Point. Count to three in your head. See what happens.

## Trick 2: The 5-Step Limit (ADHD Brains Can't Do 12-Step Mornings)

**The problem:** Most morning charts have 8-12 steps. Wake up. Make bed. Get
dressed. Eat breakfast. Brush teeth. Wash face. Pack bag. Find shoes. Put on
jacket. Don't forget lunch. Did you brush your teeth? I said brush your teeth.

**What's actually happening:** ADHD brains have limited *working memory* — the
mental scratchpad that holds "what am I doing right now?" When you present 10
steps, the brain doesn't prioritize. It freezes. The kid stares at a sock for
14 minutes not because they don't know what to do, but because they can't
*select* what to do next.

**The fix:** Reduce the morning to 5 steps. Maximum. This isn't about being
lazy — it's about fitting inside the working memory window. Once 5 steps become
automatic (usually 2-3 weeks), you can add a sixth.

Here's the 5-step template that works for most ADHD kids:

1. ⏰ **Get up** (feet on floor)
2. 👕 **Get dressed** (clothes laid out the night before)
3. 🥣 **Eat** (one breakfast option, not a menu)
4. 🪥 **Brush teeth** (visual timer next to the sink)
5. 🎒 **Grab bag** (by the door, packed last night)

That's it. Five things. If they can do those five, the morning is a win.
Everything else (make bed, wash face, find the permission slip) is bonus.

**Try this tomorrow:** Look at your current morning routine. Count the steps. If
there are more than 5, cut them. Yes, really. Pick the five that absolutely
must happen. Let the others go for now.

## Trick 3: The Bad Morning Reset (Stop the Spiral)

**The problem:** The morning goes badly. You yelled. They cried. Nobody ate
breakfast. The bus was missed. And now the entire day is ruined — for both of
you. You carry the guilt. They carry the shame. Tomorrow morning starts with "I
hope today is better" and ends the same way.

**What's actually happening:** ADHD kids (and adults) experience *emotional
dysregulation* — feelings hit harder and last longer. A bad morning isn't just
inconvenient. It colors the entire day. And when every morning starts with
anxiety about "is today going to be another bad one," the bad ones become a
self-fulfilling prophecy.

**The fix:** The Bad Morning Reset. Three steps. 90 seconds. Do it before you
leave the house (or in the car, or at the breakfast table):

1. **Name it.** "OK, that was a tough morning. That happens sometimes." No
   blame. No lecture. Just acknowledgment.

2. **Reset the clock.** "The morning is over. The rest of the day is new. We're
   starting fresh right now." This sounds simple but it's neurologically
   important — you're closing the loop so the bad morning doesn't bleed into
   the afternoon.

3. **One positive to carry.** "One thing that went well: you got dressed by
   yourself." Even on the worst morning, there's one thing. Name it. It's the
   antidote to shame.

**Try this tomorrow:** If the morning goes sideways, don't lecture. Don't
punish. Don't bring it up again at dinner. Do the 90-second reset and close
the book. Tomorrow is genuinely new.

## What These Three Tricks Have in Common

None of them require your kid to "try harder."

None of them are punishment-based.

None of them are sticker charts that work for three days and then die.

They all work WITH the ADHD brain — its need for visuals, its limited working
memory, its big emotions — instead of fighting against it.

If you try these for a week and mornings get noticeably easier, you might be
ready for the full system.
